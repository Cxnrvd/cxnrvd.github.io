Security analyst at the Uganda National Computer Emergency Response Team & Coordination Centre. Offensive security engineer. MSc postgraduate researcher at the University of East London.

I'm a Security Analyst at NITA-U (the National Information Technology Authority of Uganda), where I spend my days trying to keep critical government infrastructure ahead of APTs, ransomware crews, and the usual opportunistic noise. Most of the work doesn't show up on a compliance checklist — it's adversary simulations, forensic deep-dives, and the kind of thing that only really matters when something has already gone wrong.

On the offensive side I build and run Red Team infrastructure end-to-end — the C2 stack, the phishing layer, the post-exploitation playbook — for engagements that span web, cloud, and hybrid environments. The bugs that actually move an engagement forward usually aren't the ones an automated scanner picks up; they're logic flaws, trust assumptions, and the kind of architectural decisions you only catch by sitting with a system long enough.

When something gets through, I switch hats and do the incident response. That means showing up after the breach, working backwards through whatever the attacker left behind — process trees, registry artefacts, packet captures — and reconstructing how they moved. I've also run full ransomware recoveries: triaging what's encrypted, salvaging what isn't, and getting systems back online without re-introducing the foothold.

I speak when I have something worth saying. Past sessions with ISC2 Uganda, UIBFS, and NITA-coordinated programmes have covered RaaS economics, threat-actor communications, phishing tradecraft, and a few related rabbit holes. Longer writeups live at cxnrvd.hashnode.dev — recent posts dissect the Qilin affiliate engagement I worked, a Mallox sample that pivoted in through MS-SQL, and the ALFA Shell kit.

On the side, I'm an MSc researcher at the University of East London — looking mostly at Active Directory attack paths and adversary infrastructure detection.

If you want to chat about red-team tooling, incident response in resource-constrained environments, or some TTP cluster that's been on your mind lately — reach out.
